Defense Knowledge Base

QR Phishing, or "Quishing", is a social engineering attack where malicious actors replace legitimate QR codes (such as payment codes at dining tables, parking ticket machines, or digital flyers) with fraudulent stickers. When scanned, these malicious codes redirect targets to fake login portals, banking interfaces, or malware links designed to hijack credentials and steal funds.

Our analysis framework runs scanning rules using weighted criteria out of a maximum score of 100:

  • Missing SSL Encryption (HTTP): +25 points
  • Known Link Redirection Shortener (e.g. bit.ly): +20 points
  • Deceptive Keyword Spoofing (e.g. pay-paypal.xyz): +25 points
  • Listed Phishing Registries Match: +40 points
  • Unknown/Untrusted Domain Authority: +10 points

The total threat index places links into Safe (0-20), Medium (21-50), Suspicious (51-80), or Dangerous (81-100) buckets.

Simply scanning a QR code using modern devices will not download malware directly, as standard web sandboxes isolate initial payloads. However, QR payloads can initiate dangerous automated behaviors, like connecting to an unencrypted public Wi-Fi network that captures your data packets, pre-drafting SMS confirmations to premium bill lines, or tricking you into installing sketchy app APK packages directly from hidden files.
